Comment 2.2: How do the hyper-parameters, e.g. of the CRP, effect the performance?

Feedback: The effects of hyper-parameters are usually complex in most models. Larger or smaller parameters don't mean better performance. For example, CRP factor controls the number of semantic vectors of each entity. Larger CRP factor means more semantic vectors. Larger or smaller number of semantic vectors will decrease the performance. And too large number (too big CRP factor) also results in increasing training time. We test many values on valid sets and show the optimal in the paper like previous works do.

Comment 2.3: How sensitive is the approach as the completeness/reliability of the type information changes?

Feedback: We also note this issue. In order to mitigate their effects, we use a special intersection (eq4) to construct relation-type sets. Because relation types related to many entity type sets, the completeness/reliability of types may have less impact. And we also complement entity-type sets with these more reliable relation types.
Besides, most previous works consider training data as the ground truth. And it is a good idea to improve the robustness of model.

Comment 2.4: It seems that the authors are using CRPs as a pure generative process, after fixing all the parameters of the model (as opposed to inferring them from data, as one would do when using such a model). I did not quite follow this due to the lack of clarity here. Doesn't that mean each run will generate different number of vectors for an entity? Did authors run their model multiple times to see the effect of this? Also, the difference between this part of the model and TransG was not clear to me.

Feedback: CRP determines if an entity needs a new vector in training process. If current entity vectors do not match the triple and current entity semantics is not similar to relation, a new vector is generated and labeled relation types as its semantics. CRP in TransT considers both type-based semantics and vector-based likelihood. TransG only considers the latter. The number of semantic vectors may be a little different for each run. However, their effect on performance is little. This is a feature of probabilistic models.

Feedback: Original triples mean the triples in testing datasets (before replace). Consider a testing (original) triple-(Bob, gender, boy), when we predict the head entity of this triple, we replace the Bob with other entity which exists in the entity set, such as Mike, Tim. The “filter” settings mean removing these triples already existing in datasets from the prediction results. The filter measure is widely used in previous works.
